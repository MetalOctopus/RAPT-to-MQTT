How does this work?

The problem: Your temperature controller has a probe sitting in the fridge air. When you set it to 17.5°C, it heats or cools until the air reaches 17.5°C and then stops. But beer has far more thermal mass than air — 20 litres of liquid changes temperature much slower than the air around it. The controller thinks it's done while your beer is still degrees off target. It can take days to naturally equilibriate.

The solution: Your hydrometer (e.g. TILT) is floating inside the beer. It knows the actual liquid temperature. We use that as the source of truth and adjust the controller's target to compensate for the difference.

The maths: Every 5 minutes, we calculate:

  • Error = your beer target − actual beer temp (from hydrometer)
  • Adjustment = error × 1.5 (gain)
  • New controller target = your beer target + adjustment

So if your beer target is 17.5°C and the hydrometer reads 19.2°C, the error is −1.7°C. Multiply by 1.5 gain = −2.55°C adjustment. The controller target gets set to 15.0°C — deliberately overshooting cold to pull the liquid down faster. As the beer approaches 17.5°C, the error shrinks, the overshoot shrinks, and the controller target converges back to 17.5°C. We also clamp to safe bounds (0–35°C) and only send an update if the change exceeds 0.3°C (deadband), to avoid hammering the RAPT API with micro-adjustments.

Why the overshoot matters — the hysteresis deadzone: Your controller has a hysteresis setting (e.g. cooling hysteresis of 0.5°C). This means the compressor won't kick in until the air temperature rises above the target by that amount. If we set the controller target to exactly your beer target (17.5°C), and the fridge air is already sitting at 17.4°C, the compressor will never fire — the air is within hysteresis, so the controller thinks everything is fine, even though the liquid is still 19°C. By overshooting the target lower (e.g. 15.0°C), we ensure the air temperature is far enough above the controller's target to actually trigger a cooling cycle. The gain multiplier is what creates this overshoot — the bigger the error, the further we push past target to guarantee the hardware acts.

Why not learn a calibration constant? A static offset between fridge air and beer liquid would be simpler, but it doesn't work in practice. The relationship changes constantly — it depends on fermentation activity (yeast generate heat), ambient temperature, how full the fridge is, the volume and surface area of the fermenter, whether the door was opened, and even the beer's specific gravity. A calibration constant learned from one brew would be wrong for the next, and wrong within the same brew as conditions change. Instead, we measure the actual error right now and correct for it every 5 minutes. This is standard proportional servo control — the same approach used in industrial process control, HVAC systems, and PID loops everywhere. We just skip the I (integral) and D (derivative) terms because the 5-minute sample rate and the controller's own hardware delays (compressor cooldown, hysteresis) make them more likely to cause oscillation than help.

Your controller's own limits still apply. If it has a compressor cooldown of 5 minutes, it physically cannot start a new cooling cycle faster than that. If it has a heating hysteresis of 0.3°C, it won't kick in until the air drifts that far below target. We can't override these — they're hardware safety limits set in the RAPT Portal. What we can do is make sure the target the controller is chasing actually reflects where the beer needs to be, not just where the air is.

About RAPT2MQTT

Version ...

RAPT2MQTT is an open-source bridge between Kegland RAPT brewing devices and MQTT, built for homebrewers who want real control over their fermentation.

This project was born from frustration with cloud-only brewery management. Your data should live on your network, in your Home Assistant, under your control.

What You Get -- Free

RAPT Temperature Controller

Full cloud API integration -- monitor temperature, target, mode (heating/cooling/idle), PID tuning, all from your own dashboard.

Multi-TILT Hydrometer Support

Live specific gravity and beer temperature from TILT hydrometers via MQTT. Multiple TILTs, multiple brews, no problem.

HACS / Home Assistant Sensors

Every metric published to MQTT is ready for Home Assistant auto-discovery. Temperature, SG, ABV, mode -- all as native sensors.

Custom MQTT Notifications

Brew reminders, SG stability alerts, and day-based triggers -- all pushed to your MQTT broker for Home Assistant automations.

Smart Temperature Feedback

Cascaded control that uses the hydrometer (in-liquid) temperature to adjust the fridge controller target. Nail your beer temp, not your air temp.

Brew Session Tracking

Multi-brew management with fermentation charts, OG/FG/ABV tracking, brew logs, needle gauge, and full event history.

Built By a Homebrewer

This isn't a corporate product. It's one person, one fridge, one hydrometer, writing code between brew days. Every feature exists because I needed it for my own fermentation.

If you want an integration for gear I don't own -- I need to buy it, brew with it, understand it, and then write the code. That's where the community funding comes in.

RAPT2MQTT

Bridge between RAPT (Kegland) brewing devices and MQTT for Home Assistant and other home automation platforms.

Getting Started

  1. Go to Config and enter your MQTT broker address and port.
  2. Enter your RAPT email and API Secret.
  3. Set the Poll Interval (default 300s).
  4. Click Save Configuration, then Start the bridge.
  5. Devices appear in the sidebar as they are discovered.

TILT Hydrometer Integration

RAPT2MQTT displays live TILT data if it publishes to your MQTT broker on the TiltPi topic.

TiltPi + Custom MQTT Node

  1. Open TiltPi Node-RED at http://tiltpi.local:1880
  2. Find the ui_template node for your TILT color
  3. Add a function node, then rbe, then mqtt out to topic TiltPi

Alternative: TiltBridge (ESP32)

Flash TiltBridge firmware onto an ESP32 for native MQTT output.

Brew Reminders & Notifications

Set reminders on active brews (e.g. "Dry hop on day 5"). When triggered, a notification is published to your configured MQTT topic. Set up a Home Assistant automation to pick up RAPT2MQTT/notify messages and send push notifications.
